Executive summary

KFC enters the second half of 2026 as Australia's strongest-performing large QSR brand — record franchisee revenue, accelerating same-store sales, and the industry's best brand-health scores — but it does so in a market that is barely growing in real terms, where every major rival is investing heavily in value menus, network expansion and digital channels.

Australia's fast food & takeaway services industry reached A$29.7 billion in 2025–26, but revenue grew just 0.3% in the year and only 1.4% annualised over five years — a mature, share-driven market.[2] Within it, the takeaway chicken category KFC dominates is worth A$6.3 billion across ~1,972 businesses.[3] KFC's 817 restaurants make it the third-largest food chain in the country, behind Subway (1,256) and McDonald's (1,078).[4]

Collins Foods, KFC's largest Australian franchisee (295 of the ~817 restaurants), reported record FY26 results: revenue up 7.6% to A$1,241.3 million, same-store sales up 2.7%, underlying EBITDA of A$237.1 million, and digital sales reaching 43.2% of revenue.[5] Management attributes momentum to product innovation (Zinger Banh Mi, Upside Down Double), a reset of the delivery fee structure, kiosk rollout, and sustained brand-health leadership across brand index, buzz, satisfaction and recommendation.[5]

The strategic tension for leadership is threefold. First, the value war is escalating: McDonald's launched a three-tier McSmart range (A$6.95–9.95) on 1 July 2026, Hungry Jack's holds the entry-price crown with its A$3.00 BBQ Cheeseburger, and Guzman y Gomez sells a A$12 mini meal — while KFC's entry point sits at the A$4.95 Go Bucket.[11][12] Second, the delivery channel is consolidating into an Uber Eats–DoorDash duopoly after Menulog's November 2025 exit, shifting negotiating power toward platforms.[14] Third, labour costs are ratcheting up — award wages rose 4.75% on 1 July 2026, the third consecutive above-inflation increase.[17]

The report's central recommendation: KFC Australia should press its current advantage by attacking new dayparts (breakfast and late-night, together 36% of the QSR market and its fastest-growing segments), defending a sharpened value architecture anchored on Go Bucket and family feasts, and rebalancing delivery economics toward owned digital channels — while sustaining the 7–10 new-restaurant annual build rate and the Kwench beverage rollout already planned for FY27–28.[5][6]

Market landscape

A A$29.7 billion industry that has stopped growing in real terms — which makes share, dayparts and channel mix the only games that matter.

2.1  Size, growth and structure

IBISWorld sizes Australia's Fast Food & Takeaway Food Services industry (ANZSIC H4512) at A$29.7 billion in 2025–26, across 28,219 businesses. Growth has decelerated sharply: just 0.3% in 2025–26 and a 1.4% CAGR over 2021–26, with competition described as "high and increasing."[2][2b] Burgess Rawson's mid-2025 industry review notes one fast food outlet for every 994 Australians and cites Euromonitor forecasts of roughly 32% growth in the quick service market over the five years to 2029 — growth that will be fought over by both incumbents and new entrants.[7]

The chicken segment is the industry's second great pillar after burgers. IBISWorld's Takeaway Chicken Shops category is worth A$6.3 billion (2026) across ~1,972 businesses, having compounded at 2.8% over 2020–25 — faster than the broader industry — with Yum! Restaurants Australia, PAGAC (Chicken Treat) and Collins Foods named as its largest players.[3] Chicken's appeal — a cheaper, versatile protein with strong family-sharing formats — has insulated the category from the worst of cost-of-living trading-down, and consumers increasingly swap pricier full-service dining for QSR occasions.[8]

2.2  Share of demand: a two-brand market with a restless tail

Two datasets frame the market's concentration. Consumer research firm Nature measured share of QSR spend in 2023: McDonald's at 54%, KFC at 22% and Hungry Jack's at 12% — McDonald's reach built on convenience and range.[9] On a revenue-share basis, Burgess Rawson puts McDonald's at 23.2% of national fast food revenue.[7] Either way, the structure is consistent: McDonald's is the category's gravitational centre, KFC is the clear number two — and the number two position is precisely where competitive pressure concentrates, from burger chains discounting downward and from fast-growing challengers (Guzman y Gomez, Wendy's) scaling upward.

Store-count rankings tell a subtler story: Subway (1,256) and McDonald's (1,078) lead on footprint, but KFC (817) out-earns most larger networks per site — Collins Foods' 295 KFC restaurants alone generated A$1.24 billion in FY26, implying network sales per restaurant well above A$4 million.[5][4] And KFC is still building: it opened its 800th Australian restaurant (Oran Park, Sydney) in September 2024 — with KFC South Pacific calling itself "the fastest growing quick-service restaurant in Australia over the past six years."[4b] GapMaps' 2025 network census confirms the momentum: 2025 was the sector's strongest year of net store growth in a decade (+250 stores across the top 36 brands: 359 openings, 109 closures), and KFC led all major QSR brands on net growth (+29), ahead of McDonald's (+25) and Subway (+18); the top five QSR brands are now within 3 km of 82% of Australians.[4c]

2.3  New entrants are raising the competitive temperature

Statista's 2025 market review flags a wave of US brands entering or circling Australia: Wendy's opened its first Australian restaurant in January 2025, while Popeyes, Chuck E. Cheese and Firehouse Subs have announced intentions; In-N-Out ran a Sydney pop-up in June 2025.[8] Burgess Rawson likewise credits new entrants such as Wendy's as a driver of its 32% growth forecast — intensifying competition "among established brands."[7] Popeyes is the most strategically significant for KFC: a direct fried-chicken assault from a sister-benchmark brand. The implication is that KFC's product-quality and brand-love moat must be defended before the entrant gains beachhead scale, not after.

So whatFor KFC leadership, market maturity means growth must be manufactured — through daypart expansion (breakfast, late-night), new occasions (beverages via Kwench), share capture in chicken, and network densification — rather than ridden on category tailwinds.

KFC Australia today

Record revenue, best-in-category brand health, and a digital mix that has nearly tripled its share of sales in three years.

3.1  Ownership and operating structure

KFC's Australian network of ~817 restaurants operates under a mixed ownership model. ASX-listed Collins Foods is the largest franchisee with 295 restaurants (FY26),[5] Yum! Brands' local subsidiary (Yum! Restaurants Australia / KFC South Pacific) owns and operates around 75 equity restaurants, and the remaining ~425 restaurants are operated by independent franchisees — a spectrum that runs from groups operating as many as ~115 restaurants down to individual owner-operators with a single site (ownership structure per KFC Australia, July 2026).[4] Collins' public filings therefore provide an unusually transparent window into roughly 36% of the brand's Australian system — its economics, margins and channel mix are the best available public proxy for the wider network.

Strategically, Collins has simplified to become a pure KFC operator: in FY26 it agreed to exit Taco Bell, transferring 20 restaurants to a Yum!/Restaurant Brands Australia joint venture and closing seven, while expanding its German KFC footprint via the Munich acquisition.[6][5] For KFC Australia leadership this matters because the brand's largest franchise partner is now financially and managerially single-focused on KFC.

3.2  Financial trajectory: FY24–FY26

FY26 (53 weeks ended 3 May 2026) was a record year: KFC Australia revenue of A$1,241.3 million (+7.6%), same-store sales growth of +2.7% with second-half momentum outpacing the first half, and underlying EBITDA of A$237.1 million. Eight new restaurants opened against one closure and one relocation, taking the Collins footprint to 295; 33 remodels (including 3 "supercharged") were delivered.[5] The recovery is clear against FY25's trough (+0.3% SSS, margin compression to 19.3%) — driven by product innovation, operational excellence, commodity deflation in chicken and potatoes, and the delivery fee reset, partially offset by deliberate investment in consumer value.[10][5]

Early FY27 trading is strong: total KFC Australia sales up 6.7% in the first eight weeks of FY27.[1] Collins plans 7–10 new Australian restaurants in FY27 and remains on track for 28–30 new builds by 2028.[6][10]

Exhibit 3.1 — KFC Australia (Collins Foods) performanceCollins Foods FY24–FY26 results

3.3  Digital and brand health: the two compounding assets

Digital channels — app, kiosks and delivery — accounted for 43.2% of KFC Australia sales in FY26, up from 34.2% in FY25 and 29.4% in FY24, driven by app adoption, kiosk rollout to 100+ restaurants, and delivery growth after the fee-structure reset.[5][10] On brand, KFC "continues to dominate its QSR peers on the brand metrics that matter most" — brand index, buzz, satisfaction and recommendation — and holds the highest QSR brand index in the market.[5][10] Independent research adds texture: 76% of Australians say KFC has the best-tasting hot chips, and KFC ties McDonald's at 60% on perceived value for money, just behind Hungry Jack's 61%.[9]

Marketing has modernised around the "FLG" (Finger Lickin' Good) platform and a run of traffic-driving innovations — Zinger Banh Mi, Upside Down Double, Hot & Crispy Wrap in FY26; Waffle Double Chicken and Zinger Nachos in FY25 — which management credits with attracting new and lapsed users.[5][10]

Exhibit 3.2 — Digital share of KFC Australia salesCollins Foods FY24–FY26 results

3.4  The FY27–28 agenda already in motion

Three growth programs are publicly committed. Kwench by KFC — Yum!'s global beverage platform — rolls out nationally across FY27–28, adding a new category occasion. A breakfast trial in 16 Gold Coast restaurants and expanded late-night hours target the dayparts that made up 36% of the Australian QSR market in 2025 and grew fastest (breakfast +7%, late-night +11%). And the network keeps building at 7–10 restaurants a year.[5][6] Group capex of A$80–100 million is earmarked for FY27, weighted to Kwench, new restaurants and asset modernisation.[6]

The competitive set

Four distinct threats: McDonald's scale machine, Hungry Jack's value aggression, Guzman y Gomez's growth economics, and a restless chicken tail.

4.1  McDonald's Australia — the scale and value benchmark

McDonald's operates 1,078 restaurants and holds roughly 23% of national fast food revenue, with over half of consumer QSR spend in Nature's survey.[4][7][9] It is mid-way through its largest expansion since the 1990s — ~A$1 billion committed, targeting well over 1,000 restaurants by 2027 — with the stated goal of putting most Australians within a five-minute drive of a store.[7] On 1 July 2026 it refreshed its value architecture into three McSmart tiers (Saver A$6.95 app-only, Meal A$7.95, Plus A$9.95), replacing the 2025 price promise.[11] McDonald's competes on ubiquity, breakfast (McCafé) dominance, and an app ecosystem KFC must match — its Loose Change entry pricing undercuts every KFC item except promotional sliders.

4.2  Hungry Jack's — the value aggressor

Competitive Foods' Hungry Jack's (Burger King's Australian arm) runs 489 restaurants and takes ~12% of QSR spend.[4][9] It is the market's most aggressive entry-price player: the Penny Pinchers menu anchors at a A$3.00 BBQ Cheeseburger and A$3.75 Chicken Royale, with Super Stunner bundles (burger + chips + drink + nuggets + mini Drumstick) from A$9.15 and a relentless cadence of app deals and vouchers.[12] Notably, HJ has been expanding its fried-chicken range (Jack's Fried Chicken tenders, saucy ribs, Chicken Royale promotions) — a direct encroachment on KFC's protein turf — while consumers rate it the market's best value for money (61%).[12][9]

4.3  Guzman y Gomez — the growth outlier KFC should study

GYG is the standout growth story in Australian QSR. FY25 global network sales rose 23% to A$1.18 billion; the Australia segment (incl. Singapore/Japan) reached A$1,168.5 million with +9.6% comparable sales — triple KFC's FY26 SSS rate — from 250 segment restaurants (224 in Australia at end-FY25, growing to ~250 in Australia by May 2026 with 32 new openings in FY26).[13][13b][13f] Its unit economics are formidable: drive-thru restaurants average A$6.7 million in annual volume at ~22% restaurant margins, franchisees earn a median 50% ROI, owned digital is 19.5% of network sales, and a 100+ restaurant Australian pipeline plus 24/7 daypart expansion (23 restaurants round-the-clock by Q1 FY26) point directly at late-night and drive-thru occasions KFC is also targeting — a focus sharpened by GYG's May 2026 decision to close all eight US restaurants and concentrate capital on Australia.[13b][13c][13d][13f] GYG proves the market will reward premium-priced, fresh-positioned QSR at scale — and it is now competing for the same drive-thru sites, the same late-night daypart, and the same value conversation (A$12 Chicken Mini Meal).[15]

4.4  The chicken tail and everyone else

Red Rooster (325 restaurants, Craveable Brands stable alongside Oporto and Chicken Treat) remains the #2 chicken brand but has been network-shrinking and suburban-focused; it punches below its heritage weight.[4] Oporto and Nando's hold flavour-niche positions (Portuguese flame-grilled; peri-peri) with modest scale. Domino's (720 stores) contests delivery occasions and value bundles rather than chicken directly.[4] Subway (1,256 stores) has the largest footprint but a structurally different occasion set.[4] The sharper medium-term threat is Wendy's (entered January 2025) and especially Popeyes, whose arrival would put a global fried-chicken specialist — with Yum!-benchmark economics — directly against KFC for the first time in decades.[8]

Pricing & the value war

Every major chain has rebuilt its value architecture since 2025. KFC's ladder starts higher than its burger rivals — by design, but with consequences.

5.1  The 2026 value ladder

Cost-of-living pressure has made entry price points the industry's primary battleground. Hungry Jack's owns the bottom rung — a A$3.00 BBQ Cheeseburger and A$2.50 medium chips on its permanent Penny Pinchers menu.[12] McDonald's rebuilt its mid-tier on 1 July 2026 with McSmart at A$6.95 / A$7.95 / A$9.95.[11] KFC's entry architecture sits one rung up: the A$4.95 Go Bucket (chicken + chips) and A$3.75 sliders (A$2 on promotion), scaling through Crunch Bowls (A$9.45) and boxes to the A$14.95 Zinger Box and A$28.95–49.95 family feasts.[16][16b] GYG's A$12 Chicken Mini Meal shows even premium brands now maintain a value outpost.[15]

Exhibit 5.1 — Flagship value offers across chainsMenu prices, 2025–26 · Frugal Feeds, KFC menu, GYG, McDonald's

5.2  Reading KFC's price position

KFC's structure reflects a deliberate choice: cede the sub-A$4 snack rung to the burger chains and defend the A$5 "real food with protein" rung, where the Go Bucket offers materially more food than a A$3 cheeseburger. Consumer data supports the positioning — KFC ties McDonald's at 60% on value-for-money perception despite higher entry prices, and leads decisively on food-quality drivers (76% best chips).[9] The risk is asymmetry in the family basket: HJ's Super Stunner (A$9.15) and McDonald's McSmart Meal (A$7.95) undercut KFC's boxes, pushing KFC's value defence toward multi-person feasts (Value Feast A$28.95) where chicken's sharing economics are strongest.[12][16b]

Two pricing dynamics deserve board attention. First, commodity deflation created room: Collins cited deflation in chicken and potato inputs through FY25–26 as a margin support, funding "investment in value for consumers" without further EBITDA erosion — that tailwind is flagged as flat-to-modest for FY27, meaning future value investment must come from productivity.[10][6] Second, channel price integrity: delivered menu prices run materially above in-store (DoorDash-listed HJ Whopper at A$11.75 vs A$9.50 in-store) and KFC's FY26 delivery fee-structure reset proved pricing architecture in delivery is now a managed P&L lever, not an afterthought.[18][5]

Consumer & channel trends

A delivery duopoly is born, dayparts are the growth frontier, and digital has become the default ordering behaviour.

6.1  Delivery: the Menulog shock and the new duopoly

Menulog ceased Australian operations on 26 November 2025 after 20 years, signing a commercial agreement to redirect its customers to Uber Eats.[14][14b] Pre-exit shares were roughly Uber Eats 54%, Menulog 24%, DoorDash 15%; the market now resolves to an Uber Eats–DoorDash duopoly, with Uber the clear leader.[14c] Analysts warn of upward pressure on commissions and consumer prices, and reduced restaurant leverage; Simon-Kucher's Mitchell Taylor advises restaurants to respond with direct ordering, loyalty programs and white-label partnerships to retain control of pricing, data and promotions.[14b]

For KFC the duopoly cuts both ways: platform concentration raises commission risk, but KFC is one of very few brands with enough volume to negotiate bespoke terms — and its FY26 delivery fee-structure reset already delivered SSS benefit.[5] A further wrinkle: the TWU's minimum-pay agreement with Uber Eats and DoorDash (November 2025) begins formalising gig-economy labour costs, likely firming delivery fees over time.[14]

Exhibit 6.1 — Delivery market shares at Menulog's exitIBISWorld estimates via The Conversation, Nov 2025

6.2  Dayparts: where the growth actually is

The single most actionable consumer datum for KFC: breakfast and late-night together represented 36% of the Australian QSR market in 2025, growing at 7% and 11% respectively — the fastest-growing dayparts in the industry — and KFC is materially under-indexed in both.[5] McDonald's owns breakfast through McCafé; GYG is scaling 24/7 trading (23 restaurants) and cites after-9pm and breakfast as comp-growth drivers.[13d][13e] KFC's response — the 16-restaurant Gold Coast breakfast trial and extended late-night hours from FY27 — is correctly aimed; the strategic question is speed and scale of rollout.[5]

6.3  Digital behaviour and the trading-down consumer

Digital ordering is now the default for a large minority of occasions: KFC Australia's digital mix hit 43.2% of sales in FY26.[5] GYG's owned-app share (19.5% of network sales) shows the prize in owned channels versus aggregator rails.[13b] Meanwhile the cost-of-living consumer is not disappearing from QSR — they are trading down into it from full-service dining, and then trading across menus toward bundles and app deals; Statista notes Australians "increasingly trade in pricier full-service dining experiences for traditionally cheaper QSR meals."[8] Nature frames the opportunity as a "lipstick effect": affordable small luxuries — exactly the KFC occasion.[9]

So whatThe channel mix decision is now a board-level economic question: every point of sales shifted from aggregator delivery to owned app/kiosk preserves margin and first-party data. GYG's 19.5% owned-digital share is the benchmark to beat.

Cost & regulatory environment

Labour is the structural squeeze: three consecutive above-inflation award increases, plus rising compliance exposure on rostering and breaks.

7.1  Wages: a ratchet that only turns one way

From 1 July 2026, modern award minimum wages rose 4.75% and the National Minimum Wage rose 5.97% to A$26.44/hour (A$1,004.90/week) — the first time the weekly minimum has exceeded A$1,000.[17][17b] This follows 3.5% (2025), 3.75% (2024) and 5.75% (2023) — a cumulative ~19% increase in the award floor over four years, concentrated in accommodation & food services, one of the four sectors employing two-thirds of all award-reliant workers.[17][17c][17d] The FWC is additionally phasing out the lowest C13 classification over three cycles, compressing junior/entry pay structures upward.[17b] Collins explicitly flags labour inflation as "elevated" in FY27 even as commodity inflation flattens.[6]

Exhibit 7.1 — National Minimum Wage and award increasesFWC Annual Wage Reviews 2023–2026

7.2  Compliance and other watch items

Labour compliance is now a direct P&L and reputational risk: Collins Foods settled a class action over 10-minute rest breaks in FY26 (A$7.3 million impact within A$11.6 million of non-trading items) alongside wage-compliance estimate changes.[5][6] The same FWC-union environment that produced the delivery minimum-pay deal will keep spotlighting QSR rostering.[14] On inputs, Collins enjoyed deflation in chicken and potato costs through FY25–26 but guides to flat-to-modest commodity inflation in FY27 — removing the tailwind that funded value investment.[10][6] Kilojoule menu-labelling (displayed across all KFC menu boards) continues to normalise nutrition transparency, favouring chains that can offer credible lighter formats such as KFC's Crunch Bowls.[16]

Strategic recommendations

Six moves for FY27–FY29 — sequenced from defending the core to building new occasions.

1
Win the daypart war at trial speed, not pilot speed

Breakfast and late-night are 36% of the market and its fastest growers, and KFC is under-indexed in both.[5] The 16-store Gold Coast breakfast trial should be run as a time-boxed scale decision — predefined success gates (incremental transactions, cannibalisation, labour model) and a national go/no-go within 12 months — because McDonald's McCafé moat and GYG's 24/7 build-out mean the window to claim "chicken for breakfast/late-night" is finite.[13d] Late-night hours extension is lower-risk and should precede breakfast nationally.

KPI: daypart sales mix; incremental transactions per store; trial-to-decision cycle time.
2
Sharpen the value architecture without joining the sub-$4 race

KFC's A$4.95 Go Bucket is the right anchor — a protein-led entry meal the burger chains cannot match at A$3 — but the mid-ladder has gaps against McSmart Meal (A$7.95) and HJ Super Stunner (A$9.15).[11][12] Recommended: a permanent A$7.95–8.95 solo bundle (burger/wrap + chips + drink) to close the McSmart gap, keep sliders at promotional A$2 as the traffic spike, and make family feasts (from A$28.95) the loudly advertised centrepiece of dinner value, where chicken's per-person sharing economics beat every burger rival.[16b]

KPI: value-perception tracker (close 1pt gap to HJ's 61%); mix of entry-tier transactions; feast attachment at dinner.
3
Rebalance digital toward owned channels before the duopoly reprices

Post-Menulog, aggregator commissions have only one direction.[14c] KFC should set an explicit owned-vs-aggregated target within its 43.2% digital mix — GYG's 19.5% owned-app share proves the ceiling is high — using app-exclusive value (extend the McSmart-style app-only logic with Go Bucket bundles), loyalty mechanics, and white-label delivery to keep platform reach without platform economics.[13b][14b] Simultaneously, use KFC's scale to negotiate bespoke national terms with both platforms and A/B-test delivery-fee structures as Collins did successfully in FY26.[5]

KPI: owned-channel % of digital sales; delivery contribution margin per order; app MAU and order frequency.
4
Build the labour-cost productivity engine now

With award wages up ~19% cumulatively since 2023 and the C13 phase-out compressing entry rates, labour is the structural margin threat of the decade.[17][17b] The answer is throughput per rostered hour: extend kiosks beyond the current 100+ restaurants, kitchen automation and AI-driven rostering to demand curves, and multi-skilling to cover daypart extensions without net-new labour hours. Embed proactive compliance auditing (breaks, rostering) as a control system — the FY26 class-action settlement shows the cost of getting this wrong.[5]

KPI: sales per labour hour; kiosk order share; zero repeat compliance findings.
5
Densify the network with format flexibility

McDonald's is spending A$1bn to be within five minutes of most Australians; convenience is its moat.[7] KFC's counter is format elasticity: sustain the 7–10 build rate, prioritise drive-thru and drive-thru-only formats on smaller footprints (which fit infill sites HJ and Macca's can't use), and contest the same arterial sites GYG's 100+ store pipeline is chasing.[6][7][13f] Site selection should explicitly model cannibalisation of Red Rooster's shrinking suburban trade.[4]

KPI: new-build cash-on-cash returns; % of population within 10-minute drive; drive-thru mix of new builds.
6
Fortify chicken leadership before Popeyes lands
